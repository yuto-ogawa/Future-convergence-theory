### Abstract


This paper investigates the structural contradictions emerging from the rapid advancement of generative AI technologies, focusing on copyright evasion strategies and systemic exploitation of legal voids. Through a comprehensive analysis of Sora2, a high-performance video generation model, the study reveals how technological acceleration has been strategically deployed ahead of institutional regulation, thereby exploiting the grey zones of copyright and liability. The paper theorizes how such strategies are not incidental but structurally induced by capitalist optimization logics, where responsibility is deliberately diffused, legal transparency is obstructed, and national governance mechanisms are rendered obsolete.
 
The work is structured around a multi-layered critique that includes:
• The invisible internal processes of AI training and data handling
• Legal and ethical responsibility shifting frameworks
• The capitalist incentive to exploit regulatory lag
• The systemic erosion of state power and political credibility
• The structural inevitability of social and international conflict
• A theoretical proposal for a future society based on semantic meaning and future-causal alignment
 
The study concludes by proposing a three-layered social design model—Meaning, Institution, and Technology (MIT Model)—which reorders the current priority of development, advocating for technology to be subordinated to ethical meaning and future-directed convergence. Rather than merely critiquing existing AI strategies, the paper offers a concrete pathway toward a structurally stable and ethically grounded post-capitalist information society.
