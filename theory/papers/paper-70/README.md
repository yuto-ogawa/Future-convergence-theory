### Abstract


This paper proposes that racism does not arise primarily from historical, cultural, or political causes, but from the binary grammatical structures embedded within language itself. In particular, it argues that Indo-European languages—especially English—enforce categorical identity through grammatical mechanisms that transform descriptive attributes into essentialized identities, thereby enabling the persistence and reproduction of racial classification.

Drawing upon Future Convergence Theory (FCT), this work reconceptualizes identity as a future-oriented semantic gradient rather than a fixed categorical entity. Within this framework, identity is modeled as a dynamic function of meaning density, not as an intrinsic or biological property. The paper introduces Future-Causal English (ENΛ), a proposed linguistic architecture in which binary identity formation is structurally impossible. In ENΛ, racial categorization cannot emerge because the language itself lacks the grammatical operators required to generate fixed identity classes.

By reframing racism as a linguistic-structural phenomenon rather than a moral or sociopolitical one, this study offers a novel pathway toward eliminating racial discrimination—not through ethical enforcement, but through the redesign of linguistic and cognitive systems. The proposed framework integrates insights from linguistics, philosophy, and mathematical formalism, presenting a new paradigm in which human difference is expressed as semantic diversity rather than categorical division.
