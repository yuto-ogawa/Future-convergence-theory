### Abstract


This paper proposes a redefinition of constraint structures within Future Convergence Theory (FCT), providing a unified framework for handling ideation, comparison, and phase formation in creative processes, meaning generation, and decision-making. While conventional approaches treat constraints as external limitations or conditions, this study reconceptualizes them as perceptual fixations that eliminate alternative possibilities at the level of interpretation.

Based on this definition, comparison is no longer an explicit evaluative operation but an emergent phenomenon that occurs when entities enter the same phase of interpretation. Likewise, convergence is redefined not as optimization or selection among alternatives, but as the elimination of non-viable interpretative paths, resulting in a uniquely determined trajectory.

The paper further demonstrates that phase formation, constraint structures, interference minimization, and linguistic fixation can be understood as different representations of the same underlying mechanism. By integrating these elements into the generative stage, the proposed framework establishes a system in which comparability and demand alignment are inherently guaranteed without relying on post-hoc evaluation.

This work extends the mathematical formulation of FCT by providing an operational interpretation of constraint manifolds as structures that define non-realizable regions in semantic space, thereby bridging abstract field-theoretic models and practical generative processes.
