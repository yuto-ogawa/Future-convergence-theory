### Abstract



This paper explores how modern society structurally disables ethical sensitivity to irreversible decisions, especially those concerning life and death, such as war and vaccination. It argues that instant indicators—short-term metrics like social approval or fear mitigation—distort perception and suppress autonomous reasoning in the face of irreversible consequences.

Using Future Convergence Theory (FCT) and the Λ-System as foundational frameworks, the paper reconstructs a model of ethical judgment that begins from irreversibility and converges toward future-affirmable meaning. It introduces Λ(T), a semantic tensor of future causality, as a core component enabling individuals to anchor decisions in long-term meaning density rather than immediate environmental pressure.

By comparing judgment delegation in war and vaccination, the study reveals how ethical responsibility and semantic continuity are disrupted when individuals transfer agency to external structures. The Λ-System provides a recursive, future-oriented ethical apparatus that allows decisions to remain meaningful—even in irreversible contexts.

Ultimately, this work claims that true ethical judgment begins not from current consensus, but from the structural recognition of what cannot be undone. The creator is not one who is later proven right, but one who assigns meaning at the point of irreversibility itself.
