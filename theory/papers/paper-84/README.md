### Abstract


This paper critiques the assumption that natural language is the final carrier of thought, premises, and meaning, and independently defines a prior layer—the Premise Extraction Layer—as a theoretical object in its own right. The point of departure is the view that premises do not originally exist as sequences of propositions, but as semantic fields containing high-dimensional constraint structures, responsibility histories, contextual bindings, exception conditions, and unarticulated suspended components. Because natural language projects these into a one-dimensional linear sequence, it inevitably produces compression distortion, order distortion, granularity collapse, and contextual stripping. Therefore, in order to design an OS-like structure capable of supporting premise updating and high-density thought operations, it is necessary to define a layer that preserves, extracts, and re-projects pre-linguistic semantic structures before attempting to improve language itself.

This paper defines this layer as the Premise Extraction Layer and introduces six basic elements: (i) semantic field, (ii) constraint set, (iii) phase-preserving representation, (iv) linear linguistic projection, (v) re-injectability, and (vi) semantic-density retention rate. It further argues that premises are not summaries of sentences but extractions of operational constraints, that natural language is not the premise itself but a purpose-dependent view projected from a phase-preserving representation, and that silence, suspension, and undefinedness are not defects but constitutive components of meaning. The paper concludes that designing beyond language does not mean escaping into the non-linguistic; rather, it means demoting language to one final output form and fixing the semantic field itself as the higher-order object of design.
