### Abstract


High-density internal representations—such as those experienced in dreams, intuition, insight, or immediately after awakening—are often subjectively coherent and logically structured. However, when translated into language, they rapidly undergo abstraction, fragmentation, and subject fixation.

This paper formulates this phenomenon not as a mere failure of memory, but as a structural transformation problem arising from the projection of nonlinear, parallel semantic structures onto a linear and sequential descriptive space.

Building on the previously proposed Dual Fixed-Point Theory, where meaning inflow I constrained by a future fixed point and a simultaneous fixed point generates internal representations via a projection operator Π and a state update operator G, this study focuses on the subsequent stage of transformation. We introduce a sequential projection operator L, which maps implicit internal representations R_imp to explicit linguistic representations R_exp.

Through this framework, we demonstrate that the difficulty in verbalizing dreams and intuitions does not stem from an inability to construct them internally, but rather from the absence of a reconstruction protocol compatible with sequential expression.

Furthermore, we formalize key properties of internal representations—parallelism, high semantic density, subject non-differentiation, temporal nonlinearity, and implicit logic—and analyze how these properties induce projection loss during linguistic generation.

Finally, this paper presents a unified theoretical framework—the connection problem between nonlinear semantic space and sequential descriptive space—applicable across domains including dreams, intuition, narrative creation, cognitive science, and AI language generation.
