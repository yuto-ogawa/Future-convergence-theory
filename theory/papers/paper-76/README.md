### Abstract



This paper develops a structural theory of order, meaning, ethics, and free will based on a future-causal worldview, starting from the proposition that it is possible to meaningfully ask which future should be chosen. It argues that such a question can only arise within a worldview in which causality is not exclusively past-directed, but instead allows future structures to influence present choices.

The paper first analyzes historical modes of order formation. In ancient civilizations, particularly exemplified by ancient Egypt, meaning functioned as a justificatory layer supporting a pre-given cosmic or political order. In modernity, mythological justification was replaced by rationality, optimization, and numerical governance; however, these systems gradually absolutized themselves, causing humanity to lose awareness that order is a constructed structure rather than a natural necessity.

To overcome this limitation, the paper introduces a future-causal framework in which the present is shaped by a set of possible futures. Within this framework, it defines the concept of a future fixed point: a future structure that attracts present choices while preserving openness, inclusivity, and self-corrective capacity. A “good” future is not defined morally or ideologically, but structurally, by its ability to avoid the reduction of possibilities and the exclusion of participating agents.

Based on this model, ethics is redefined not as a system of norms but as directional alignment toward selected future fixed points. Free will is reconceptualized as the capacity to choose among future branches rather than liberation from past causes, and responsibility is understood as accountability for the future structures one continuously selects.

The paper concludes by arguing that order should not be preserved as a static object but treated as a field that is continuously updated through future-oriented questioning. Only civilizations capable of asking about their futures can meaningfully possess one. This work presents the theoretical foundation for such a civilization.
